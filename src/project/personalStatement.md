My undergraduate experience has been defined by a shift from software development toward low-level systems and hardware-oriented computing. While I began college interested in animation and design, I quickly recognized that my strengths lay in building the systems behind the tools I once used. This transition led me to computer science, and ultimately to electrical and computer engineering concepts, where I developed a strong interest in the interaction between software and hardware, particularly in parallel systems and performance-oriented computing.

Alongside my coursework, I worked as a developer for the University of Arizona College of Nursing, where I led the design and implementation of a bilingual health resource platform used by schools across Arizona. As the sole developer on the project, I was responsible for translating stakeholder requirements into a functional system, designing the architecture, and maintaining performance and accessibility standards. This experience required me to think beyond isolated code and instead focus on system-level design, user constraints, and long-term maintainability. Working within a grant-funded environment also exposed me to the structure of applied research and reinforced my interest in contributing to technically grounded, real-world projects.

My technical interests continued to develop through both coursework and independent projects. I found that I was most engaged in classes involving computer organization, digital logic, and systems programming, where I could work closer to the hardware abstraction boundary. In parallel, I applied these concepts in projects that integrated machine learning and data processing, including a sustainability prediction model that used regression techniques to analyze long-term urban metrics. These experiences strengthened my interest in parallel and distributed computation, particularly in how hardware-aware optimization can improve performance in data-intensive systems.

I also contributed to research presented at the International Telemetry Conference, where I worked on a team exploring the integration of machine learning into animatronic attention systems. In this role, I developed code to support our experimental approach and helped translate technical concepts into presentations that could be understood across disciplines. This experience reinforced my ability to connect theory with implementation and to communicate complex systems clearly, both of which are essential in collaborative engineering environments.

While balancing academic work with professional responsibilities and family obligations affected portions of my academic record, I addressed gaps directly, including retaking Systems Programming and earning an A. This experience strengthened my foundation in C and reinforced my ability to recover and perform in technically rigorous environments.

At Oregon State University, I am particularly interested in advancing my understanding of parallel computing systems and hardware-software interaction, with a focus on GPU programming and performance optimization. I am drawn to OSU’s emphasis on applied research and its work in high-performance computing and computer architecture, which aligns with my goal of developing efficient, scalable systems that bridge software and hardware.

I am seeking graduate study to deepen my technical foundation and to position myself for work in areas such as high-performance computing, embedded systems, or hardware-accelerated machine learning. My background in software development, combined with my growing focus on low-level systems, has prepared me to contribute meaningfully to this field. Oregon State’s program offers the environment and technical direction necessary for me to continue that progression.
